DNA Structure — The Double Helix

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the genetic material in most organisms. Watson and Crick proposed the double helix model in 1953, based on Rosalind Franklin's X-ray diffraction data.

Key Components
A nucleotide = Phosphate group + Deoxyribose sugar (5-carbon) + Nitrogenous base. Nucleotides are linked by 3'–5' phosphodiester bonds.

Nitrogenous Bases — Chargaff's Rules

Chargaff's rule: [A] = [T] and [G] = [C]. Therefore A+G = T+C (purines = pyrimidines).

Two strands are antiparallel. Diameter = 2 nm. Pitch = 3.4 nm per turn (10 bp)

Key Structural Features

  • Antiparallel strands — one runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'
  • Right-handed helix — B-form is most common in cells
  • 3.4 nm per turn — each turn has 10 base pairs; each bp = 0.34 nm
  • Diameter = 2 nm — consistent throughout the molecule
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone is on the outside; bases inside (hydrophobic core)
  • Major and minor grooves — proteins bind DNA at these grooves
NEET High Yield
Human haploid genome has ~3.2 × 10⁹ bp. Total DNA per cell (diploid) = ~6.4 × 10⁹ bp. Length = ~2 metres. Histone proteins (H2A, H2B, H3, H4 — octamer; + H1 as linker) package DNA into nucleosomes. 146 bp of DNA wraps around each nucleosome core (1.65 turns).

DNA Replication

Meselson and Stahl (1958) proved semi-conservative replication using ¹⁵N/¹⁴N density gradient centrifugation. Each new DNA molecule has one original strand and one newly synthesized strand.

Origin of Replication
In E. coli: single origin (oriC). In eukaryotes: multiple origins (thousands), allowing faster replication of large genomes. Replication is bidirectional from each origin.

Key Enzymes in Replication

Leading vs Lagging Strand

Leading Strand

Synthesized continuously in 5'→3' direction towards the fork. Needs only one primer.

Lagging Strand

Synthesized discontinuously away from the fork as Okazaki fragments. Each fragment needs its own primer. Fragments are ~1000–2000 nt in prokaryotes; ~100–200 nt in eukaryotes.

NEET Trick
DNA Pol cannot initiate new chains — it can only extend. That's why RNA primer is needed. DNA synthesis is always 5' → 3'. The template is read 3' → 5'.

Transcription

DNA → RNA. The process of copying genetic information from DNA into mRNA. Only one strand of DNA (the template/antisense strand, read 3'→5') is transcribed.

Transcription in Prokaryotes

  • Single RNA Pol (core enzyme: α₂ββ'ω; holoenzyme with σ factor) does all transcription
  • σ (sigma) factor recognizes the promoter region (Pribnow box: -10 region; -35 region)
  • RNA Pol moves 3'→5' on template, synthesizes RNA 5'→3'
  • Rho-independent termination: hairpin loop + poly-U sequence
  • Rho-dependent: Rho protein unwinds RNA-DNA hybrid

Post-Transcriptional Processing (Eukaryotes)

hnRNA → mRNA Processing
1. Capping — 7-methylguanosine cap added to 5' end (protects from exonucleases; helps ribosome recognition)

2. Polyadenylation — poly-A tail (~200 A residues) added to 3' end (stability + nuclear export)

3. Splicing — introns removed, exons joined by spliceosomes (snRNPs). Mature mRNA leaves nucleus.

Translation (Protein Synthesis)

mRNA → Protein. Occurs at ribosomes in cytoplasm. Uses aminoacyl-tRNAs, requires energy (ATP + GTP).

Stages of Translation

  • Activation — Amino acids attached to tRNA by aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase (uses ATP → AMP)
  • Initiation — Small subunit binds mRNA at Shine-Dalgarno sequence (prokaryotes) or 5' cap (eukaryotes). Start codon = AUG (Met). Large subunit joins.
  • Elongation — A site: incoming aminoacyl-tRNA; P site: peptidyl-tRNA; E site: exit. Peptide bond by peptidyl transferase (ribozyme activity of 23S rRNA). Ribosome translocates 3'.
  • Termination — Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA. Release factors cause polypeptide release.
Polysome / Polyribosome
Multiple ribosomes translating a single mRNA simultaneously = polysome. Increases efficiency of protein synthesis significantly.

Genetic Code

The rules by which nucleotide triplets (codons) specify amino acids.

Properties of Genetic Code

  • Triplet — 3 nucleotides = 1 codon; 4³ = 64 possible codons
  • Degenerate — most amino acids have multiple codons (except Met and Trp)
  • Non-overlapping — each nucleotide is part of only one codon
  • Non-ambiguous — each codon specifies only one amino acid
  • Comma-less — no punctuation between codons; read continuously
  • Universal — same in almost all organisms (exceptions: mitochondria, mycoplasma)
  • AUG — start codon (codes for Met; formyl-Met in prokaryotes)
  • UAA, UAG, UGA — stop codons (non-sense codons; no tRNA for them)
Important codons to memorize
AUG = Start (Met) · UAA = Ochre · UAG = Amber · UGA = Opal/Umber
UGG = Trp (only codon for Trp) · Met (AUG) and Trp (UGG) are the only non-degenerate ones.

Hybridization

Hybridization is the intermixing of atomic orbitals of comparable energy to form new hybrid orbitals with equivalent energy and shape.

HybridizationOrbitals MixedShapeBond AngleExamples
sp³1s + 3pTetrahedral109.5°CH₄, CCl₄, ethane
sp²1s + 2pTrigonal planar120°Ethylene (C₂H₄), benzene
sp1s + 1pLinear180°Acetylene (C₂H₂), CO₂
sp³ — Methane (CH₄)

Carbon forms 4 equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals. All C–H bonds are sigma (σ). No pi bonds. Free rotation around C–C bonds. Each H–C–H angle = 109.5°

sp² — Ethylene (C₂H₄)

Each carbon: 3 sp² orbitals (σ bonds) + 1 unhybridized p orbital. The p orbitals overlap sideways → 1 π bond. C=C has 1σ + 1π. Molecule is planar.

sp — Acetylene (C₂H₂)

Each carbon: 2 sp orbitals (σ bonds) + 2 unhybridized p orbitals. Two p–p overlaps → 2 π bonds. C≡C has 1σ + 2π. Molecule is linear.

Bond Character Rule
Single bond = σ only · Double bond = 1σ + 1π · Triple bond = 1σ + 2π
Rotation is free around σ bonds but restricted around π bonds (causes geometric isomerism in alkenes).

Inductive Effect & Bond Polarity

  • +I effect (electron-donating): alkyl groups — order: C(CH₃)₃ > CH(CH₃)₂ > CH₂CH₃ > CH₃
  • –I effect (electron-withdrawing): –NO₂ > –CN > –COOH > –F > –Cl > –Br > –I
  • Electronegativity order: F > O > N > Cl > Br > C > H

IUPAC Nomenclature

International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry rules for systematic naming of organic compounds.

Steps for IUPAC Naming

  • Step 1: Longest chain — find the longest continuous carbon chain (parent chain)
  • Step 2: Principal characteristic group — the highest priority functional group gets lowest locant
  • Step 3: Numbering — number from the end nearest the principal group
  • Step 4: Substituents — name alphabetically (ignore prefixes di/tri/etc.)
  • Step 5: Assemble — substituents (alphabetical) + parent + suffix

Isomerism

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements or spatial orientations.

Structural Isomerism
  • Chain isomers: differ in carbon skeleton (e.g., butane vs isobutane)
  • Position isomers: differ in position of substituent/double bond
  • Functional group isomers: differ in functional group (alcohol vs ether)
  • Metamerism: differ in alkyl groups on either side of same functional group
  • Tautomerism: keto-enol tautomers (dynamic interconversion)
Stereoisomerism
  • Geometrical (cis-trans): requires restricted rotation (double bond) + two different groups on each C
  • Optical isomerism: non-superimposable mirror images (enantiomers); requires chiral carbon (4 different groups)
  • Conformational: different spatial arrangements via bond rotation (not true isomers)

Cis-Trans Examples

2-butene
cis-2-butene: both CH₃ groups on same side → higher boiling point (more polar)
trans-2-butene: CH₃ groups on opposite sides → more stable (less steric strain)

Optical Isomerism Key Points

  • Chiral carbon (asymmetric carbon): C with 4 different substituents — marked with asterisk (*)
  • Enantiomers: non-superimposable mirror images; opposite optical rotation (+/–)
  • Racemic mixture (±): equimolar mixture of enantiomers; optically inactive
  • Diastereomers: stereoisomers that are not mirror images; different physical properties
  • Meso compound: has chiral centres but internal plane of symmetry; optically inactive
NEET Formula
Max optical isomers = 2ⁿ (n = number of chiral carbons) · For meso compounds, actual = less than 2ⁿ

Markovnikov's Rule

Rule
In addition of HX to an unsymmetrical alkene, the H adds to the carbon with more hydrogens (hydrogen-rich carbon), and X adds to the carbon with fewer hydrogens.

Why? Electrophilic addition goes through the more stable carbocation intermediate (3° > 2° > 1° > CH₃⁺).
